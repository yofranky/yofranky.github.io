How I Think About Community

Not a fictional board — four real lenses I've actually operated from across a career spent in both infrastructure engineering and community leadership. Click any card for the full perspective.

🛠️

The Engineer

8 years as primary VMware engineer, SanMar

"A community without monitoring is just hope."

Reliability Root Cause Analysis Systems Thinking

For four years I was the only VMware engineer responsible for infrastructure running 1,000+ VMs, and P1 incidents were hitting weekly. What fixed it wasn't heroics — it was root cause analysis on every recurring pattern and proactive monitoring that surfaced problems before they became outages.

I apply the same discipline to community health. Engagement dips, chapter attendance drops, contributor churn — these have root causes too, and they're findable if you instrument for them instead of reacting to the loudest complaint. Treat community like production infrastructure: measure it, harden the weak points, and stop firefighting the same fire every week.

🌱

The Volunteer Leader

Seattle VMUG revival & Regional Champion, VMUG

"Growth isn't recruiting more people — it's giving the people already there a reason to come back."

Grassroots Growth Mentorship Chapter Building

I inherited a Seattle VMUG chapter running one meeting a year. I didn't fix it by finding new people — I fixed it by giving the existing ones a reason to show up seven times instead of once: real programming, actual relationships, and leadership that shared ownership instead of hoarding it.

That earned a nomination as U.S. West Regional VMUG Champion, where I mentored 60+ volunteer leaders across the western US. The job wasn't telling them what to do — it was building playbooks so a first-time chapter leader could launch something good without reinventing everything from zero.

🤝

The Inclusion Advocate

Diversity & Inclusion Councilmember, VMUG

"The next great contributor might be intimidated by the first message they'd have to send."

Accessibility Belonging Diverse Voices

Two years on VMUG's Diversity & Inclusion Council taught me that most communities don't lose people to hostility — they lose them to quiet intimidation. Nobody says "you don't belong here." The jargon, the assumed context, the unwritten rules just make someone decide it's not worth finding out.

I worked on speaker recruitment practices, inclusive programming, and mentorship pathways built specifically to close that gap — not as a compliance checkbox, but because the health of a community shows up first in who's missing from it.

📊

The Business Partner

Community Program Manager, Broadcom (23,000+ members)

"If community can't show up in a business review, it doesn't get protected when budgets tighten."

Executive Alignment Program Continuity Data-Driven Ops

I ran VMware's global developer community program — 23,000+ members across Discord, WordPress, and forums — through the Broadcom acquisition, one of the most disruptive periods a community like that can face. Platforms, ownership, and priorities all shifted at once, and the program had to survive it with contributor trust intact.

That only works if community metrics speak the same language as the rest of the business — engagement tied to retention, programming tied to roadmap influence. A 1,000-signups-in-48-hours exam-prep launch is a great story, but it only protects the program's budget if leadership can see the line from that number to something they actually care about.
