The Whole Picture

Not just a resume. This is the story behind the career — the moments, the lessons, and the controlled chaos that shaped who I am.

🎰

The Vegas Years

My first day at the Bellagio, I started on a Friday on swing shift as the floating breaker — that meant Pit 3 (Craps tables) and Pit 2 (high stakes Blackjack and Roulette). I learned the hard way what "sink or swim" really means. Every number, every chip, every transaction had to be tracked perfectly in a regulated environment where mistakes had consequences. There was no gentle ramp — just a deep end and a clock.

As a bar back, I entered a whole new world. I was never a big lover of alcohol, but I loved the camaraderie of the team. I loved meeting new faces, working different stations, restaurants, bars, and shifts — graveyard, swing, day. It was humbling to scrub and clean bars and take out the trash while I saw others never touch those things and make a lot more money. But it pushed me. It made me want to use my brain instead of my back. I knew Las Vegas wasn't the long-term plan, and that was okay — it was fuel.

At the ARIA, my personality let me flourish. Running full-speed through the hallways with my cart full of luggage wasn't just about tips — it was about earning that 5-star, 5-diamond service award. I learned that genuine care for people, clear communication, and discretion in high-stakes guest situations translates to any room you walk into — whether it's a casino floor or an executive briefing.

🖥️

The Pivot

I moved to Washington state and turned to IT. I was terrified to get on the phones at the support desk. I thought everyone knew what they were doing and had all the answers. I was so glad to find out they were normal — and it didn't take me long to get up to speed and get promoted to the engineer role.

That promotion brought me back down to reality. Suddenly I was the sole VMware engineer responsible for 1,000+ VMs, and P1 incidents were hitting almost every week. I could never go anywhere without my laptop because I was always on-call. It was a stressful life — but it taught me more in a few years than any classroom could have.

And that's when I started volunteering to help others so they wouldn't feel alone, like I did. I went all in as a volunteer, just like I've done my whole life. I put as much work into community as I did into my day job — because I remembered what it felt like to be the only one carrying the weight, and I didn't want anyone else to feel that way.

🔥

Controlled Chaos

"I love controlled chaos. Whether it's a Friday night rush on the Strip, a P1 at 2 AM, or a community navigating an acquisition — the pressure focuses me. It actually calms me down."

There's a through-line that connects every chapter of my career, and it took me a long time to see it clearly: I thrive when the pressure is on, and I'm at my best when I'm helping other people through tough moments.

In the kitchen at 2 AM during a Friday rush, behind a luggage cart sprinting through ARIA hallways, sitting alone at 2 AM with a P1 and 1,000 VMs depending on you, standing in front of 23,000 community members during an acquisition — the scenario changes, but the feeling doesn't. The chaos focuses me. It strips away everything unnecessary and leaves only what matters: the problem and the people who need help with it.

🏨

Hospitality

The foundation. I learned that service isn't a transaction — it's a mindset. Whether you're carrying luggage or carrying a team through an outage, the principles are the same: show up, communicate clearly, and care about the outcome.

⚙️

Engineering

The discipline. Root cause analysis, proactive monitoring, documentation-first. I learned that fixing the root cause of a recurring problem is always better than being the hero who can fix it fast. That applies to systems — and to people.

🤝

Community

The purpose. I've found that community leadership isn't a soft skill — it's infrastructure. The same way you'd monitor a server cluster, you monitor community health. The same way you'd harden a system, you strengthen trust. It's all the same work.

🌱

Why I Keep Growing

I strive to surround myself with people better than me. It pushes me to stay sharp and reach the next level. That's why I left SanMar after eight years — I was a big fish in a small pond, and I wanted to feel small again.

The layoff was tough. But it gave me something unexpected: time with my family as my kids grow up, and space to learn different technologies as I prep for interviews that sometimes come and sometimes don't. That's led me here, and it's taught me something important: I love helping people — not only people, but tech people.

Tech is a natural fit because it's a never-ending growth path. There's always someone sharper than you, always a problem you haven't solved yet, always a community of like-minded people who will push you to be better. That's what I want — to keep learning, keep helping, and keep feeling that productive discomfort of being the smallest fish in the pond again.

📍

Where I Am Now

In my current role at the Washington State Department of Health, I help people — sometimes an 80-year-old trying to navigate a tech-heavy license renewal process. I bring that calm voice over the phone, create emails with as many pictures as possible to make it easy. Other times I feel like my talent is being wasted, because I know I can do so much more. But then I remember: helping people through tough moments is the thread that runs through every chapter of my career.

I'm open to opportunities where I can help both people and tech people. Where I can bring the whole self — the engineer who stabilized 1,000+ VMs, the community leader who guided 23,000 members through uncertainty, the former bellperson who still believes service is a mindset. I want to feel small again. I want to be around people who push me. I want to build, to mentor, to lead, and to keep learning.
